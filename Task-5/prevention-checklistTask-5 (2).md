Stay Safe Online – Cybercrime Prevention Checklist
For College Students in India

---

Prevention Checklist

1. Use strong, unique passwords  
   Make sure they are a mix of case, numbers, and symbols, and please, please, please don't use the same password everywhere. If one of your accounts is compromised, don't risk the others.

2. Enable Two-Factor Authentication  
   Turn on 2FA for email, social media, and banking app accounts. It takes 10 seconds to do this, but it can save a lot of trouble later on, even if the attacker already has your password, they won't be able to get in.

3. Don't share your OTP, not even with bank officials  
   (Financial/UPI Safety) This is a serious one. No bank, UPI app, or financial institution will ever call you asking for your OTP, UPI PIN, or CVV. If they do, hang up. It's a scam.

4. Verify before hitting "Pay" on UPI  
   Financial/UPI Safety Just two seconds of caution can save a lot of trouble. Make sure to check the recipient's name and UPI ID before paying. Scammers can create UPI IDs that are very close to the real ones. And remember, you don't need to share your PIN to receive money. If anyone asks you to, they're a scammer.
5. Stay off public Wi-Fi for anything sensitive
   free Wi-Fi at a cafe or hostel feels convenient, but it's an easy place for someone to intercept your data. Use your mobile data or a VPN when logging into anything important.

6. Pause before you click on any link
   If a message promises a prize, a job, or asks you to "verify your account" slow down. Don't click the link. Go directly to the official website yourself. Phishing links are designed to look real.

7. Keep your personal life off public social media
   Your phone number, home address, college schedule — none of that needs to be public. Oversharing makes it easy for scammers to build a profile on you and target you specifically.

8. Only download apps from official stores
   Stick to the Google Play Store or Apple App Store. If someone sends you an APK file over WhatsApp or a random link, don't install it — it very likely contains spyware or malware.

9. Check which apps have access to what
   Go through your app permissions once in a while. Does a calculator really need your microphone? Does a game need your contacts? Revoke anything that doesn't make sense.

10. Log out when using someone else's device
    After using a college computer lab, library system, or a friend's phone — log out, clear the browser history, and remove any saved passwords. Don't leave a door open behind you.

11. You don't have to face online harassment alone
    If you are being harassed, threatened, or bullied online, don't be afraid to talk to someone you trust. It’s not your fault, and it’s never embarrassing. There are people who are there to help you.

12. Be careful with the information you share about your daily activities
    Remember, if you share your location, your activities, and your company with everyone, you are basically giving strangers too much information. Remember to adjust your privacy settings.

13. Be careful with online dating
   Remember to check your dates before you meet them in person. You should video call them, let a friend know where you are going, and trust yourself.
Report Cybercrime Immediately:

| Channel | Details |
| 🌐 National Cyber Crime Portal | [cybercrime.gov.in](https://cybercrime.gov.in) |
| 📞 Helpline Number | 1930 (available anytime for financial cyber fraud) |

#Quick tip: If you've been scammed on UPI or lost money online, call 1930 as soon as possible. The faster you report it, the better the chance of freezing the transaction and recovering your money.
Honestly, what surprised me most while researching this topic was realising that cybercrime doesn't just happen to people who are careless or unfamiliar with technology. College students, young professionals, even people who work in IT — they all fall for scams. That was unsettling. The attacks aren't always technical. A lot of them are psychological scammers are good at creating urgency, fear, or excitement, and they count on you reacting before you think.
The habit I am personally going to change is slowing down with UPI payments and being more careful with emails and messages. I used to just confirm transactions without really looking. Now I'll take those extra few seconds to check the name, verify the UPI ID, and actually read where an email is coming from before clicking anything. It sounds small, but that pause could make a real difference.
